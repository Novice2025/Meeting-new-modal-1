Approach 1: Technical Presence

The "Audio Quality" Trap

The science of HEAR vs. LISTEN

HEAR: Signal strength (Physiological - ability to receive - it is natural and automatic).
LISTEN: Mental focus (Action - it requires conscious effort - requires close attention).

Key expressions deep dive:
  • You're coming through okay: High-end professional communication used in professional settings by executives. ("estou ouvindo você bem").
  • Yes, I can hear you: too basic. ("Estou escutando / ouvindo você").
  • Loud and clear: Professional game changer - high confidence confirmation - used in all situations.
Situation: Standard Audio Check

"Can you (all or everyone) hear me okay before we start?"

You: "Yes, I can hear you clearly (Basic to intermediate)."

You: "Alright John, you are coming through clearly (Super advanced)."

Approach 2: Impactful Participation

Beyond "Participate"

Expression deep dive:

Join: Neutral entry. Use for arrivals. - E.g. Knock! Knock! at the door: May I join in the meeting? = is the same to May I come in?

Take part in: Dynamic involvement. Better for active meetings - this is the verb for 'participar em reuniões ativas'.

Get involved: Strategic commitment to a project over time ' this is the verb for 'participar sentido de envolvido profundamente'.

May I add something?: Low-friction entry to share an idea.

May I give an insight?: Low-friction entry to share an idea - common in meeting to say 'contribuir' in the sense of sharing your experience and firm opinion.

Jump in: Proactive interruption to help or clarify - common in meeting to say 'contribuir'.

Approach 5: Leadership Framing

Elite Boardroom Language

High-level professionals do not simply say okay or yes. In meetings, executives confirm information with precision, authority, and control. These expressions show clarity, confidence, and professional presence.

Duly Noted

Used when you have heard the point, recognised it, and mentally recorded it for action or future reference. Calm, professional, and powerful.

Manager: We need tighter deadlines next quarter.
You: Duly noted. We’ll adjust the timeline accordingly.

Point Taken

Used when someone gives criticism, correction, or advice and you acknowledge the message professionally.

Director: We moved too slowly on this project.
You: Point taken. We’ll speed up execution.

Understood

Direct, efficient, and unambiguous. Shows you understand clearly and are ready to proceed.

Manager: Please send the revised proposal by 3 PM.
You: Understood.

Received

Formal and concise. Often used in written communication or structured meetings to confirm information has been accepted.

Lead: I’ve shared the updated figures.
You: Received. I’ll review them now.

Copy That / Roger That

Strong confirmation used in fast-paced, technical, operational, or high-pressure environments. Clear, immediate, action-focused.

Operations: Move the launch call to 10 AM.
You: Copy that. Updating the team now.

Crystal Clear

Used when there is zero confusion. Emphasises complete understanding and alignment.

CEO: We focus on profit first, growth second.
You: Crystal clear.
CEO Communication Style

How Ramón Laguarta Speaks

Top-tier CEOs do not simply talk. They frame discussions, guide decisions, and create momentum. Their language sounds collaborative, but it also keeps authority, direction, and control.

Power Phrase 1

“Let me provide some color to those figures.”

This means: let me explain the context behind the numbers. CEOs use this when raw data alone does not tell the full story. It sounds polished, strategic, and highly executive.

Board Member: Revenue dropped 4% this quarter.
CEO: Let me provide some color to those figures. The decline was temporary and linked to supply chain delays.

Power Phrase 2

“I'd love to hear your take on the friction points.”

This means: I want your opinion on the problems, obstacles, or slowdowns. Great leaders invite input while sounding respectful and in control.

CEO: Sales growth is slowing in Latin America.
CEO: I'd love to hear your take on the friction points.
Director: Distribution delays and pricing pressure.

Power Phrase 3

“We need to pivot our strategy toward Q4.”

This means: we need to change direction quickly and intelligently. CEOs use pivot when markets shift, priorities change, or results demand action.

CEO: Consumer demand is changing faster than expected.
CEO: We need to pivot our strategy toward Q4 and focus on premium products.
Executive Influence Language

Stronger CEO Power Phrases

Elite leaders do not speak casually when pressure is high. They use precise language that creates confidence, direction, and authority. These upgraded phrases sound sharper, stronger, and more executive.

Power Phrase 1

“Let me put those numbers into perspective.”

Stronger than “provide some color.” Used when a leader wants to control the narrative and explain why the figures should be viewed differently. It sounds confident, strategic, and authoritative.

Board Member: Revenue dropped 4% this quarter.
CEO: Let me put those numbers into perspective. The decline reflects short-term logistics pressure, not reduced demand.

Power Phrase 2

“I’d value your perspective on the core friction points.”

More refined than “I'd love to hear your take.” This invites expertise, shows respect, and keeps executive authority. Excellent for senior meetings.

CEO: Sales growth is slowing in Latin America.
CEO: I’d value your perspective on the core friction points.
Director: Distribution delays and pricing pressure.

Power Phrase 3

“We need to recalibrate our strategy heading into Q4.”

Stronger and more sophisticated than “pivot.” Recalibrate suggests an intelligent strategic adjustment based on market realities. Very common in executive communication.

CEO: Consumer demand is changing faster than expected.
CEO: We need to recalibrate our strategy heading into Q4 and prioritise premium growth.
Boardroom Mastery

10 More Power Phrases

These are the phrases senior leaders use to guide meetings, control direction, invite input, and communicate with executive presence. Calm language. Strong authority.

1. Let’s align on priorities.

Used to bring everyone back to what matters most.

Situation 1:
CEO: We have too many parallel initiatives. Let’s align on priorities first.
Situation 2:
CEO: Marketing, hiring, and expansion all matter, but let’s align on priorities for this quarter.

2. We need sharper execution.

Means the plan is fine, but delivery must improve.

Situation 1:
CEO: Strategy is solid. We now need sharper execution across teams.
Situation 2:
CEO: The opportunity is there. What’s missing is sharper execution.

3. What are we solving for?

Refocuses the meeting on the real problem.

Situation 1:
CEO: Before we continue, what are we solving for exactly?
Situation 2:
CEO: We’re discussing features, but what are we solving for: growth or retention?

4. Let’s challenge our assumptions.

Encourages strategic thinking and better decisions.

Situation 1:
CEO: Let’s challenge our assumptions before approving this forecast.
Situation 2:
CEO: We assume demand will grow. Let’s challenge our assumptions first.

5. I want to pressure-test this plan.

Means testing the plan under realistic pressure.

Situation 1:
CEO: I want to pressure-test this plan before launch.
Situation 2:
CEO: Let’s pressure-test the numbers if sales drop by 10%.

6. Where are the bottlenecks?

Used to identify delays and inefficiencies.

Situation 1:
CEO: Delivery is late again. Where are the bottlenecks?
Situation 2:
CEO: If approvals take two weeks, that’s the bottleneck.

7. Let’s simplify the decision path.

Means reducing bureaucracy and speeding action.

Situation 1:
CEO: Approvals are too slow. Let’s simplify the decision path.
Situation 2:
CEO: Too many signatures are required. Let’s simplify the decision path.

8. We need momentum going into Q4.

Creates urgency and forward movement.

Situation 1:
CEO: We need momentum going into Q4, not hesitation.
Situation 2:
CEO: Let’s close key deals now. We need momentum going into Q4.

9. Who owns the next move?

Focuses on accountability and action.

Situation 1:
CEO: Good discussion. Who owns the next move?
Situation 2:
CEO: We agreed on the plan. Who owns the next move by Friday?

10. Let’s move with discipline.

Means act fast, but with structure and control.

Situation 1:
CEO: The opportunity is real. Let’s move with discipline.
Situation 2:
CEO: Speed matters, but mistakes are costly. Let’s move with discipline.
